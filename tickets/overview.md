# busTracker Roadmap

This roadmap tracks the build of a bus operator's public website: real-time
vehicle tracking, journey planning, timetables, ticket sales, and the staff
tooling behind them.

The goal is not to build everything at once. The goal is to sequence the work so
each layer is proven before the next layer is added. **The live tracker leads**
— it is the first customer-visible feature we intend to land, and the data
foundation it needs is also what the journey planner and ticketing surfaces will
sit on later.

> **This roadmap is the plan; the dashboard is the inventory.** For the exhaustive
> live status of every ticket — grouped by sprint, with state and progress —
> open [`ticket-dashboard.html`](./ticket-dashboard.html) or read
> [`queue.json`](./queue.json). Both are generated by `build_dashboard.py`; never
> hand-edit them. See the README's "Ticket dashboard" section.

# Current Product Shape

busTracker is **greenfield**. Nothing is built yet. What exists today:

- `features.md` — the full functional brief from the operator
- this ticket system, and the sprint plan below

Implementation begins only after **Sprint 0** resolves the operator, legal,
integration, repository-safety, and delivery assumptions that the original
queue left external to every ticket.

The reference implementation we are replicating for the tracker is
[ember.to](https://www.ember.to) — a full-screen MapLibre map with route
geometry drawn as a connected network, live vehicle markers, a `LIVE` status
badge, and geolocate/zoom controls, over Stadia Maps tiles.

The main risks are currently:

- **Feed quality is the whole ballgame.** The tracker is only as good as the
  vehicle-location feed behind it. We do not yet have hands-on evidence of the
  operator's real GTFS-Realtime feed quality, update cadence, or coverage.
- **No architecture exists yet**, so every contract is still cheap to change —
  and correspondingly cheap to get wrong.
- **Scope is very large.** `features.md` describes a multi-year product. Sprint
  gates exist to stop us building all of it speculatively.

# Current Reading of the Programme

The biggest open question is **whether the vehicle-location feed is good enough
to build a trustworthy tracker on**. Everything in Sprint 2 assumes it is.
Sprint 1 exists to find out before we have built a UI on top of bad data.

The second question is **how honest the tracker is about its own uncertainty**.
A tracker that silently shows a stale position as live is worse than one that
says "no live data — showing timetable". This principle is baked into the
Sprint 1 and 2 acceptance criteria deliberately, not bolted on later.

Everything after Sprint 2 remains resequenceable against commercial priorities,
but commerce, staff access, privacy, legal, and operational controls must move
with the feature they protect rather than being deferred to the final sprint.

# Ticket numbering

- Each sprint owns a hundred-block: Sprint 0 = `0xx`, Sprint 1 = `1xx`, Sprint 2 = `2xx`, etc.
- Work tickets start at `N01`. Insert mid-sprint additions at the next free number (`N06`, `N07`, ...).
- `N00` may be used for a cross-cutting foundation that must precede the sprint's feature tickets.
- **`N99` is always the sprint review gate.** It is the only mandatory ticket in every sprint and exists to force a Go/No-Go decision before moving on.

**Tickets:**
- [x] [Ticket 201: Map Shell, Tiles, and Controls](./201-map-shell.md)

**Exit criteria:**
- the map renders the route network and live vehicles on desktop and mobile

- a tracking link can be shared and opens on the right vehicle or journey
- the map is keyboard-navigable and screen-reader usable
- team decides whether the tracker is ready for public traffic

**Explicitly out of scope:** ticket sales, accounts, journey planning, operator dashboards.

# Programme Exit Criteria

The programme is successful when:

1. Passengers can track a bus in real time and trust what the map tells them.
2. Passengers can plan a journey and buy a ticket on a phone in a few taps.
3. Disruption reaches passengers before they reach the stop.
4. Staff can run fares, content, and incidents without developer involvement.
5. Integrations fail safely and visibly instead of silently.
6. The service stays up on its busiest, worst day.

# How to Read This Roadmap

- **Sprints are sequential.** Do not start Sprint N+1 until Sprint N's `N99` review gate is passed.
- **Each sprint can stop the programme.** If complexity exceeds value, the review ticket should explicitly say so. "Stop here" is a valid outcome.
- **Tickets inside a sprint can be parallelised** where dependencies allow.
- **Sprints 1 and 2 are the priority.** The live tracker is the first feature we intend to land; later sprints are real but resequenceable.
- **Never present an estimate as a guarantee.** This applies to arrival times in the product and to delivery dates on this roadmap.
